# How to Avoid Scams When Buying Polycarbonate Sheets Internationally

International PC sheet procurement fraud costs buyers an estimated $50-100M annually. The scams are predictable — and avoidable.

## Common Scam Patterns

### 1. The Certificate Fraud

**Scam:** Factory shows a real IATF 16949 or ISO 9001 certificate — but it belongs to a different factory, is expired, or is photoshopped.

**Protection:** Verify the certificate number on the issuing body's online database. For IATF 16949: IAF CertSearch (free). Check: factory name, address, scope, and expiry date match exactly.

### 2. The Bait-and-Switch Resin

**Scam:** Samples use Makrolon/Lexan resin. Production order uses cheap domestic resin. You can't tell until panels yellow in year 2.

**Protection:** Require resin lot number traceability in the purchase contract. Commission FTIR testing on random production samples ($200-300). Include "material substitution without written approval = contract void" clause.

### 3. The Trading Company Impersonation

**Scam:** A trading company claims to be the factory. They show factory photos, videos, and certificates — all from a real factory they have no relationship with. You pay the trading company; the factory never receives an order.

**Protection:** Verify the factory address on the certificate matches the production site. Commission a third-party audit that physically visits the facility. Pay the factory directly (bank account name must match factory business license name).

### 4. The Shipping Scam

**Scam:** Container is loaded with panels, bill of lading is issued — but the container is swapped before loading onto the vessel. You receive empty or filled with scrap.

**Protection:** Hire a pre-shipment inspection agent to witness container loading and sealing. Record the container seal number. Verify the seal number on the bill of lading matches.

### 5. The Deposit Fraud

**Scam:** Factory requests 50-100% deposit upfront, then disappears. Common with "too good to be true" pricing.

**Protection:** Standard international trade terms: 30% deposit, 70% against copy of bill of lading. Never pay 100% upfront to a first-time supplier. Use Letter of Credit (L/C) for orders over $50,000.

## Red Flag Checklist

- Factory cannot provide verifiable IATF 16949 or ISO 9001 certificate number
- Price is > 30% below market average
- Factory refuses third-party audit
- Payment requested to personal or offshore account (not factory's corporate account)
- Factory website is less than 1 year old, no verifiable export history
- Only communicates via WhatsApp/WeChat, no corporate email
- Pressure to close the deal quickly ("special price this week only")

## Q: Should I use Alibaba Trade Assurance?

Yes — for first orders under $10,000. Trade Assurance provides some protection, but it's not a substitute for due diligence. The real value is that it filters out the worst scammers who refuse to use it.
